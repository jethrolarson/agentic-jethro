*I notice that we've been burned by x here because y is not a thought terminating technique. Right? It's a it's a declaration of risk that the agent can verify. And if it doesn't apply or the risk turns out to be false, it can make a judgment call.*

Right — that's the mechanism that makes it not thought-terminating, worth stating precisely. "We've been burned by X because Y" is a falsifiable claim with a causal story attached. The agent can check: does Y actually hold in this instance? If yes, the risk transfers and it should act on it. If no, the risk doesn't apply, and the agent has grounds to say so — it's not defying an instruction, it's evaluating a claim and finding it inapplicable, which is exactly the reasoning step you want preserved.

"Always wrap X in Y" has no causal story, so there's nothing to verify. Compliance is the whole content of the instruction. The agent can only obey or disobey — there's no "check if this applies" step available, because the instruction never told you what the underlying hazard actually was, just what to do about an unstated one. That's why it terminates thought rather than informing it: it substitutes an action for an explanation, so there's nothing left to reason about, only something to follow or ignore.

Which means the test for whether a skill entry is teaching or scripting is pretty mechanical: does it include a "because"? Not decoratively — a "because" whose truth the agent could actually check against the situation in front of it, causal Y, not the "your team likes this pattern" filler-because. Bad procedural entries often smuggle in "because it's best practice" or "because it's more maintainable" — has the shape of a reason but isn't checkable against the specific case, so it still functions as a directive underneath the disguise.
